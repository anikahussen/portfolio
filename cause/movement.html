W A V E 1 W A V E 2 W A V E 3 W A V E 4
1848-1920

WAVE 1: the political movement

1848: Women’s Rights Seneca Falls Convention: Elizabeth Cady Stanton, among other women rewrote the Declaration of Independence in the form of the Declaration of Sentiments. This demanded the full wage, property, career, education and citizenship rights of women.

1849: Elizabeth Blackwell became the first woman to receive a medical degree.

1851: Sojourner Truth gave her “Ain’t I a Woman” speech, speaking about both the abolitionist and women’s rights movement, which movements worked together until the end of the Civil War.”

1920: The 19TH Amendment passed, giving woman the right to vote for the first time

1960-1983

WAVE 2: the social movement

1963: Betty Friedan published her book, The Feminine Mystique explaining how woman were limited at home, undereducated and not treated like the adults that they are. The Equal Pay Act of 1963, finally reflecting that women and men should receive equal pay.

1983: Many books were published by woman: Alice Walker’s first poetry collection and her well known book, The Color Purple. Toni Morrison also published her books (along with bell hooks), expressed what it meant to be a black-woman, in the male-dominated America and overlooked by white feminists.

1973: The Roe v. Wade case finally gave some semblance of control of a woman over her own body by preventing the ban of abortion.

1983-1998

WAVE 3: the individual movement

1991: At a Senate confirmation hearing, Anita Hill accused Thomas Clarence of sexual harassment. This gained national attention. Yet, Thomas was. Confirmed and Anita was discredited.

1992: Punk Band Bikini Kill published the “Riot Grrrl Manifesto”, creating a global radical feminist music genre, empowering women’s voices and speaking about violence against women and LGBTQ members.

2000-Now

WAVE 4: intersectional

Expanding on the previous wave, Fourth Wavers are fighting against the representation of women in entertainment and the media. The digital age has brought upon many movements like #MeToo, fighting against objectification and breaking “the glass ceiling”.
